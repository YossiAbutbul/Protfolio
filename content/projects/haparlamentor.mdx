## The problem

*הפרלמנט* is one of those shows whose lines get quoted constantly — but try to track down which short the line came from and you're scrolling through Mako, hunting blind. Hebrew transcripts barely exist on the open web, and even when you find one, it's a wall of text with no episode anchor.

## What I built

A Hebrew phrase-to-episode search app, in two phases.

**Phase 1 — the shell (this codebase).** A static Next.js 15 app with a CRT-styled UI: scanlines, grain, vignette, and phosphor glow over the search field. Idle hero shows a logo and a "channel input" search bar. Results render as TV-guide rows — episode tag, speaker chyron, ±1 line of context, and a deep-link out to Mako. Empty state is a no-signal static screen. All of the visual effects are gated behind `prefers-reduced-motion`, so anyone who needs the motion off gets a clean, flat layout.

**Phase 2 — the data pipeline (in progress).** A Whisper batch script transcribes Mako audio into per-episode JSON, a `build-index` script concatenates everything and emits a Fuse.js fuzzy index, and a deep-link layer maps timestamps into the Mako player.

## Highlights

- **RTL Hebrew first** — Heebo, Assistant, JetBrains Mono; bidi handled at the layout level rather than patched per component.
- **Static export** — ships as a flat bundle to GitHub Pages, no server, no API.
- **Reduced-motion-aware effects** — every CRT layer has a static fallback.
- **Mock-data dev loop** — Phase 1 ships against a hand-typed `transcripts.json` so the UI can be finished and reviewed before the transcription pipeline lands.

## What I learned

CRT effects are a stack of cheap tricks layered carefully — none of them are expensive on their own, but the order matters and `prefers-reduced-motion` has to be designed in from the first line of CSS, not bolted on. And building the UI against mock data first turned out to be the right call: it kept the Whisper/Fuse.js work decoupled from the design, and lets each phase ship cleanly on its own.
